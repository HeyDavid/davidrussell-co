Sapiens is the book everyone has read, half-read, or nods along about. I
actually finished it. Three things stuck, and one of them I now think about at
work constantly.

## Shared fictions

Harari's central claim — sapiens beat the other hominids by inventing
collective fictions (money, nations, gods, corporations) and coordinating
around them — holds up under pressure better than most big-idea theses. It
reframed my job. Brands are fictions. The useful ones make coordination
cheaper: you see the mark, you trust the work, everybody saves time. The
extractive ones spend that trust on you.

I spend part of my week documenting the extractive kind — a scam is just a
fiction with a payload — and the contrast teaches you fast which kind you're
building.

## Where he loses me

The "agriculture was a trap" chapter is intellectually satisfying and too tidy.
Yes, early farmers worked longer hours than foragers. No, that doesn't make the
transition a mistake — sometimes longer hours buy something worth having:
denser communities, specialized work, the runway to the next jump. Harari knows
this. The punchier framing just sells better. Steelman him there and the book
gets more useful, not less.

## Why read it as an operator

Most marketing advice argues tactics, one level above the landing page. Sapiens
operates several floors up: which fictions deserve strengthening, which deserve
dismantling, which to leave alone. That's closer to the actual job than most
books written about the job.

Read it slowly. Argue with it out loud. Come back in five years and argue
differently.
